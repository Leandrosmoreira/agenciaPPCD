### ROTEIRO

> Hey.
>
> It's Math.
>
> Pull up a chair.

*Pausa de 2 segundos. Imagem muda para: quintal com grill, cerca de madeira, pickup truck na entrada de uma garagem.*

> I've been wanting to do this for a long time. Probably longer than I want to admit. But I'm a shy guy. Always have been. The kind of guy who sits in the back of the room and hopes nobody asks him a question.

*Imagem: sala de aula americana genérica dos anos 80. Carteiras de madeira.*

> My wife handles the talking in this family. I just nod and agree. That's been working for twenty-three years, so I'm not gonna change it now.

**[QUEM É O MATT — 0:30 a 3:00]**

*Imagens: Texas, paisagem rural, campo aberto, estrada reta até o horizonte.*

> So here's the short version of me.
>
> I'm forty-nine years old. I grew up in a small town in Texas. My old man was career military — Army, twenty years, two tours in Vietnam. He never talked about what he saw over there. Never. Not once. The only thing he ever said about it was: "Some things stay where they happened, son."
>
> And that was that.

*Imagem: base militar americana, anos 70. Famílias, casas iguais, bandeira.*

> My mom is seventy-seven now. Still lives in Texas. Still goes to church every Sunday. And — this is the part that makes me laugh — she now has a Spotify playlist full of Def Leppard and Bon Jovi. The same woman who spent fifteen years yelling "Turn that noise off, Matthew!" now listens to "Pour Some Sugar on Me" while she waters her garden.

*Pausa.*

> Rock and roll won. Took twenty years, but it won.

*Imagem: rádio de carro AM/FM dos anos 80.*

> I've got a wife who's smarter than me and three kids. My oldest is twenty-one. My daughter is eighteen. My youngest is fifteen and is currently trying to explain to me why something called Valorant is a sport. I'm not convinced.
>
> I run a small business downtown in a college town in Missouri. Moved here from Texas about eight years ago after... well, after some things changed. We'll get to that.

*Imagem: Columbia Missouri downtown, genérica, sem identificação clara.*

> I've got a brother. Younger than me. He's in my childhood memories more than anybody except my parents. Every Saturday morning cartoon. Every trip to the arcade. Every Christmas morning. He was there. I don't talk about him much — that's his business, not mine. But you can't tell the story of my childhood without him in it.

**[POR QUE ESTE CANAL — 3:00 a 5:30]**

*Trilha muda levemente — mais melancólica. Imagens: estrada vazia à noite, neon de motel, placas vintage.*

> So why am I here? Why now?
>
> I'll tell you. But it's gonna take a second.
>
> I've been carrying stories around in my head for thirty years. Memories. Sounds. Smells. Things I can't explain to my kids because they grew up in a different world. And for thirty years, those stories just... sat there. In the back of my mind. Like boxes in an attic that nobody opens.

*Imagem: sótão com caixas empoeiradas. Luz passando por uma janela.*

> I'm not a talker. Ask anybody who knows me. Getting a full sentence out of me at dinner is a miracle. My wife says I have the emotional range of a fence post. She's not entirely wrong.
>
> But the thing is — I feel everything. I just don't know how to say it. Never did. My old man was the same way. He could fix an engine with his eyes closed but couldn't tell you he loved you if his life depended on it. I inherited that.

*Pausa longa — 3 segundos. Imagem: mão de homem trabalhando numa peça mecânica.*

> About a year ago, I found something. I started using AI — artificial intelligence — to help me write. To help me think. And I know that sounds strange. A forty-nine year old guy from Texas talking to a computer. But here's the thing: it doesn't judge. It doesn't interrupt. It doesn't look at you funny when you start talking about a car you sold twenty years ago and your eyes get wet.
>
> It just listens. And it helps me take the mess in my head and turn it into something that makes sense.

*Imagem: tela de computador com texto, desfocada. Não mostrar o que está escrito.*

> That's how this channel happened. I started typing memories into a screen. And I realized — these aren't just my memories. They're everybody's. Every guy my age who grew up in a small town, who went to the arcade on Saturday, who rented movies at Blockbuster on Friday night, who heard rock and roll for the first time and felt the world crack open.
>
> Those stories matter. And nobody's telling them. Not like this.

---

**[O QUE ESTE CANAL É — 5:30 a 7:30]**

*Trilha sobe levemente — mais esperançosa. Imagens: montagem rápida de nostalgia — arcade, NES, VHS, mall, muscle car, concert, TV de tubo.*

> So here's what this channel is.
>
> Some days, I'm gonna sit here and tell you a story. My story. About growing up in Texas with a military dad who never said "I love you" but showed it in a hundred other ways. About hearing Guns N' Roses for the first time on a borrowed cassette tape and feeling like the world just got bigger. About a car I loved. About a dream I had. About the things I lost.
>
> I call those "Pull Up a Chair." It's just me talking.

*Imagem: poltrona velha de couro num canto de sala com abajur. Silêncio.*

> Other days, I'm gonna take you back. Back to the stores that don't exist anymore. The food that tasted different. The toys you forgot about until right now. The things that made the '80s and '90s feel like a different planet.
>
> And some days — and these might be my favorite — I'm gonna tell you about the bands nobody remembers. The bands that played the Sunset Strip in 1987 and never got signed. The bands that were just as good as Mötley Crüe or Guns N' Roses but the record deal went to somebody else. I've spent years — years — tracking these bands down. Collecting their music. Learning their stories. Somebody should tell them. Might as well be me.

*Imagem: Sunset Strip à noite, anos 80. Neon, carros, gente. Foto de arquivo.*

> I was born five years too late for that scene. Five years. If I'd been born in '71 instead of '76, I would've been there. In front of the Whisky a Go Go on a Tuesday night, watching the next big thing play for fifty people. Instead, I was eleven years old in Texas, reading about it in a magazine that arrived two months late.
>
> I know everything about the greatest party in rock and roll history. Except what it felt like to actually be there.

*Pausa de 3 segundos.*

> That's the thing about nostalgia. Sometimes it's not about missing something you had. It's about missing something you almost had.

---

**[O TOM — 7:30 a 9:30]**

*Imagens: mais lentas. Paisagens do Midwest. Campos. Céu aberto. Pôr do sol.*

> Now, I gotta be honest about a few things.
>
> I'm not gonna show my face. That's just not me. You'll hear my voice, you'll hear my stories, but you're not gonna see me standing in front of a camera trying to be something I'm not. This isn't about me being pretty. It's about the stories being real.
>
> I'm not gonna talk politics. Not here. I've got opinions — everybody does. But I think we got enough people yelling at each other about that stuff. This is a place to remember. Democrats and Republicans both went to the arcade. Everybody rented from Blockbuster. We can agree on that.

*Imagem: arcade nos anos 80 — kids de diversas raças e estilos jogando lado a lado.*

> And I'm not gonna pretend the past was perfect. It wasn't. I know that. But some things about it were better. Not everything. Some things. The way a dollar used to mean something. The way a Friday night at the video store felt like an event. The way you could be a kid without a phone tracking every move you made.
>
> I'm not saying we should go back. I'm saying we should remember. Because if we don't, nobody will.

**[O PAI — 9:30 a 11:30]**

*Trilha muda — mais pesada. Piano solo, lento. Imagens: cemitério de veteranos. Bandeiras americanas nos túmulos. Nada dramático — respeitoso, sereno.*

> My old man died in 2015. He was seventy years old. Complications from Agent Orange — the stuff they sprayed in Vietnam. He spent twenty years serving this country and the country gave him a lung disease as a thank you.
>
> I was holding his hand when he went. He didn't say anything at the end. No big speech. He just... stopped. While I was telling him about my oldest son's football game. One second he was there, the next he wasn't.
>
> I didn't cry at the funeral. Matthews men don't cry. That's what he taught me.

*Pausa longa — 4 segundos.*

> Three months later, I was driving alone on a dirt road outside of Temple. Johnny Cash on the radio. "Hurt." The cover. And I pulled over, turned off the engine, and I cried for twenty minutes.

*Pausa.*

> Turns out, Matthews men do cry. They just do it alone, on a dirt road, where nobody can see.

*Imagem: estrada de terra no Texas. Horizonte vazio. Silêncio por 3 segundos antes de continuar.*

> A lot of this channel... I think a lot of it is about him. About the things he showed me without saying a word. About the world he grew up in and the world he gave me. About the conversations we never had. And maybe... maybe about having those conversations now, even though he can't hear them.
>
> Or maybe he can. I'd like to think so.

Before all that's left is a world that doesn't remember what a Blockbuster smelled like on a Friday night in 1994.
